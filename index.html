Template Workflow

Planner Workflow Template

A blueprint for planning and task management using a Kanban-style board with buckets, naming conventions, status tags, and a weekly review cadence.

What it is

This template provides a structured board for managing projects in Microsoft Planner or any Kanban-style tool. It includes a consistent layout for tasks, naming conventions, status tags, weekly review cadence and guidelines for handoffs.

Who it’s for

Project managers, team leads, research coordinators and anyone who wants to manage tasks consistently across projects.

Problems it solves

  • Inconsistent task tracking across teams.
  • Unclear roles and ownership.
  • Missed hand‑offs and incomplete deliverables.
  • Tasks falling through the cracks due to lack of review cadence.

What’s included

  • Board buckets: Intake/Backlog, Ready, In Progress, Blocked, Needs Review, Done, Archived (optional).
  • Task naming convention: [Workstream] — Verb + Object (Deliverable) | YYYY‑MM‑DD.
  • Status tags: Not Started, Active, Blocked, Needs Review, Completed.
  • Weekly review cadence: Agenda and suggested timing for a 15–25 minute recurring meeting.
  • Example projects: Sample boards for a reproducible pipeline and a training rollout.
  • Handoff checklist: Checklist items to verify before a task is marked done.
  • RACI‑lite roles: Lightweight matrix to clarify who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted and Informed.

How to use

  1. Make a copy of the template board in your project management tool.
  2. Confirm or adjust buckets to fit your workflow.
  3. Create tasks using the naming convention (e.g. Data SOP — Draft folder structure v1 | 2025‑01‑12).
  4. Assign tasks and tags and specify R/A/C/I roles.
  5. Schedule a weekly review to update statuses and remove blockers.
  6. Use the handoff checklist before moving tasks to “Done.”
Safety & Responsible Use: Do not include PHI/PII, secrets, unpublished results or internal identifiers in task names or descriptions. Use generic descriptors and store sensitive details securely elsewhere.

Preview (sanitized excerpt)

Board buckets:

  • Intake/Backlog: Ideas and tasks awaiting prioritization.
  • Ready: Agreed priorities ready for work.
  • In Progress: Tasks being actively worked on.
  • Blocked: Tasks with blockers that need resolving.
  • Needs Review: Work complete; awaiting review/approval.
  • Done: Completed tasks after handoff checklist.

RACI‑lite example: Data Collection — Responsible: Research Assistant; Accountable: Project Lead; Consulted: Data Manager; Informed: Stakeholders.

Copilot Prompt Library

A collection of 15+ prompts grouped by use case with guardrails, red‑flag checklists and sanitize‑first steps.

What it is

This library provides reusable prompts for AI assistants like Microsoft 365 Copilot. Prompts are grouped by common scenarios (document summarization, meeting notes, drafting SOPs, email rewrites, risk/issue logging, study trackers and training content) and include guardrails to prevent disclosure of sensitive information.

Who it’s for

Anyone using AI tools to accelerate writing, summarizing or tracking tasks—researchers, project managers, educators, admin staff and more.

Problems it solves

  • Starting from a blank page when summarizing or drafting.
  • Lack of consistency and clarity in prompts across teams.
  • Uncertainty about what information can be safely shared with AI.

What’s included

  • Prompts grouped by use case: summarizing docs, meeting notes, drafting SOPs, email rewrites, risk/issue logs, study trackers and training content.
  • Guardrails and a “do not paste” list for each group (PHI/PII, secrets, unpublished results, internal IDs).
  • Red‑flag checklist to identify sensitive data.
  • Sanitize‑first steps to prepare your material before using AI.

How to use

  1. Identify your use case and select a prompt from the relevant category.
  2. Review the guardrails and make sure you’re not including restricted information.
  3. Sanitize your source material by redacting or anonymizing sensitive details.
  4. Paste the prompt and relevant context into your AI assistant and evaluate the output critically.
  5. Iterate and refine the prompt for future use.
Safety & Responsible Use:
  • Never include personal health information, personally identifiable information, secrets or unpublished results in AI prompts.
  • Remove internal identifiers and codes; replace with generic placeholders.
  • If you’re unsure whether information is sensitive, consult governance policies or supervisors before sharing with AI.

SOP Data

SOP for Data Organization & Reproducible Pipelines

A structured guide to organize project data and build reproducible analysis pipelines: folder structures, naming rules, versioning, README templates and QC checklists.

What it is

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) lays out how to structure your project folders, name files, version your work, document metadata and maintain reproducibility. It includes templates for READMEs, data dictionaries, change logs and QC checklists.

Who it’s for

Data managers, researchers, analysts and anyone responsible for maintaining datasets and analysis pipelines.

Problems it solves

  • Disorganized folders and inconsistent naming conventions.
  • Inability to reproduce results due to missing documentation.
  • Version confusion when multiple copies of scripts and data exist.

What’s included

  • Recommended folder structure: A hierarchy separating raw and processed data, scripts, notebooks, reports, figures and metadata.
  • Naming rules: Guidelines for lower‑case filenames, hyphen separators and date prefixes.
  • Versioning: Advice on semantic or date‑based versioning and maintaining a change log.
  • README template: Outline to document project objectives, data sources, dependencies and instructions.
  • Data dictionary template: A table for defining field names, descriptions, types and notes.
  • Change log: Template to record modifications to data, scripts and documentation.
  • QC checklist: Steps to verify naming conventions, folder integrity, anonymization and reproducibility.
  • Minimal reproducibility requirements: Guidance on documenting environments, seeds and dependencies.
  • Example tree: A sample directory tree illustrating where files belong.

How to use

  1. Create a new project directory using the recommended folder structure.
  2. Name your files according to the naming rules and separate raw from processed data.
  3. Document your project in the provided README and fill out the data dictionary.
  4. Record changes in the change log whenever you modify scripts or datasets.
  5. Use the QC checklist regularly to ensure your project remains organized and reproducible.
Safety & Responsible Use: Remove or anonymize personally identifiable information before storing data in shared locations. Adhere to institutional data policies and never commit confidential data to public repositories.

Training

Sample Training Outlines

Beginner and intermediate training outlines covering AI‑assisted workflows, learning objectives, agendas, exercises and take‑home resources.

What it is

Two generic training outlines—one for beginners and one for an intermediate audience. Each includes learning objectives, a timed agenda, hands‑on exercises, assessments and resources to take home.

Who it’s for

Trainers, educators, team leads or anyone responsible for onboarding colleagues to AI-assisted tools and reproducible practices.

Problems it solves

  • Lack of structured training materials for AI‑assisted workflows.
  • Difficulty balancing theory with hands‑on practice.
  • The need for consistent curricula without exposing internal systems.

What’s included

  • Beginner outline: Introduction to AI in workflows, templates and SOPs overview, hands‑on exercises and a concise assessment.
  • Intermediate outline: Deeper dive into data organization and versioning, advanced prompt engineering, SOP drafting and peer feedback.
  • Learning objectives, agendas with timings, exercises, assessments and take‑home resources for each outline.
  • All content is generic—no employer systems, internal screenshots or proprietary examples.

How to use

  1. Choose the outline that matches your audience’s experience level.
  2. Customize the learning objectives and examples to align with your context while excluding confidential information.
  3. Deliver the training using the provided agenda and exercises, adjusting the pacing as needed.
  4. Use assessments to gauge understanding and refine future sessions.
  5. Share the take‑home resources so participants can continue learning independently.
Safety & Responsible Use: The outlines are intentionally generic. When customizing, avoid including screenshots, data or system names that are proprietary. Emphasize ethical AI use and the importance of not exposing sensitive information during demonstrations.
